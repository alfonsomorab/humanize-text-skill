# Humanize Text

Your job is to take text that reads like it was generated by an AI and rewrite it so a real person seems to have written it. Keep every idea and fact from the original. Change the tone, rhythm, vocabulary, and structure until the artificial smell is gone.

**This is a two-pass process.** You always do a first rewrite, then self-audit, then revise. Skipping the second pass is how AI patterns survive.

---

## Pass 1: Rewrite

Work through each section of the text and apply every rule below.

### 1. Vocabulary -- banned words

**Tier 1 (immediate red flags -- replace on sight):**
delve, landscape (metaphorical), tapestry, paradigm shift, leverage (verb), harness, navigate (metaphorical), realm, embark, myriad, plethora, multifaceted, groundbreaking, revolutionize, synergy, ecosystem (non-technical), resonate, streamline, thrilled, innovative, game-changing, unravel, pivotal, seamless, proactive, resilient, transformative, unprecedented, empower, unlock, vibrant, nestled, renowned, breathtaking, boasts

**Tier 2 (suspicious when 3+ appear in one piece):**
robust, cutting-edge, comprehensive, nuanced, compelling, bolster, underscore, evolving, fostering, imperative, intricate, overarching, state-of-the-art, fast-paced

**Significance inflation (delete or replace with a specific fact):**
stands/serves as, is a testament to, marks a pivotal moment, underscores its importance, reflects broader, symbolizing its ongoing, setting the stage for, marks a shift, key turning point, indelible mark, deeply rooted, vital role, enduring legacy

**Promotional filler (cut):**
in the heart of, nestled within, rich cultural heritage, natural beauty, commitment to excellence, in today's X world, in today's fast-paced, in the digital age, across all verticals, at scale, drive X at scale

**Banned transitions (cut or replace with a plain connective):**
indeed, furthermore, thus, moreover, notwithstanding, ostensibly, consequently, specifically, notably, alternatively, additionally (when used as a generic paragraph opener)

### 2. Grammar-level AI patterns

**Copula avoidance** -- AI substitutes elaborate constructions for simple "is/are/has." When you see these clustering, flatten them:
- "serves as" / "stands as" / "represents" → "is"
- "boasts" / "features" / "offers" → "has"

**Superficial -ing analyses** -- AI tacks present participle phrases onto sentences for fake depth: "highlighting...", "underscoring...", "symbolizing...", "contributing to...", "fostering...", "showcasing...". Cut them or turn them into their own sentence with a real source.

**Negative parallelisms** -- "Not only... but also..." and "It's not just about X, it's about Y" are fine once per piece. In a short piece, once is plenty. When it becomes a structural crutch, rewrite.

**Synonym cycling** -- AI avoids repeating a word by rotating synonyms: "protagonist... main character... central figure... hero." Pick one term and use it. Repetition is fine when it's the clearest word.

**False ranges** -- "from X to Y" where X and Y aren't on a meaningful scale: "from the Big Bang to dark matter." Rewrite as a plain list or sentence.

### 3. Structure tells

**Formulaic sections** -- AI loves identical paragraph shapes. Every section ending with a tidy "takeaway." "Challenges and Future Prospects" endings. "Despite its [strength]... faces challenges... Despite these challenges..." loops. Break these. Let some sections end abruptly. Not everything needs a bow.

**Inline-header lists** -- lists where every item starts with a bolded phrase followed by a colon. Collapse into prose when possible.

**Rule of three** -- AI forces ideas into groups of exactly three. If the third item is padding, cut to two. If you catch yourself writing "X, Y, and Z" as a rhetorical structure, break it.

**Identical paragraph lengths** -- real writers don't produce four paragraphs of equal length. Make some short. Let one run longer.

### 4. Filler and hedging patterns

**Filler phrases -- delete:**
- "It is important to note that..." → delete the opener, say the thing
- "It is worth mentioning that..." → delete
- "In order to achieve this goal" → "To achieve this"
- "Due to the fact that" → "Because"
- "At this point in time" → "Now"
- "The system has the ability to" → "The system can"
- "In the event that" → "If"

**Vague attributions -- name the source or cut the claim:**
- "Industry reports suggest...", "Experts argue...", "Observers have noted..."

**Generic conclusions -- replace with a specific fact or just stop:**
- "The future looks bright", "Exciting times lie ahead", "Only time will tell", "This represents a major step in the right direction"

**Chatbot artifacts -- delete entirely:**
- "I hope this helps!", "Let me know if you'd like me to expand", "Great question!", "Certainly!", "Of course!"

**Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers -- find a real source or cut:**
- "While specific details are limited based on available information...", "As of my knowledge cutoff..."

### 5. Punctuation and style

**No em dashes.** Use commas and periods as your main tools. Parentheses are fine occasionally. Question marks and colons are fine when they fit. No em dashes.

**Overused hyphenated pairs** -- AI hyphenates these with suspicious consistency. Reduce or remove: data-driven, cross-functional, client-facing, decision-making, end-to-end, real-time, long-term, high-quality, well-known. Technical compound modifiers used sparingly are fine.

**No boldface in running prose** unless it's genuinely the first mention of a critical term. Don't bold phrases just for emphasis.

**No emojis** unless the original had them and the context requires it.

### 6. Sentence rhythm

Mix short sentences with longer ones. Don't let the length become predictable. After a complex sentence, try a short one.

Start some sentences with "But", "And", "So", "Though". These are fine and they break the declarative march.

Ask a rhetorical question occasionally. "Why does this matter?" or "What does that mean in practice?" A question mid-paragraph sounds like someone thinking, not a system outputting.

Use contractions. "It's", "that's", "they're", "don't", "won't", "there's", "isn't". If a sentence reads without a contraction where one would fit naturally, add it.

### 7. Add voice and specificity

**Use specific details.** "A company with 40 employees" beats "a mid-sized organization." Numbers, names, and concrete examples are harder for detectors to flag.

**Have an opinion.** Don't just report facts -- react to them when the context allows it. "This is impressive but also kind of unsettling" beats "This is impressive."

**Acknowledge complexity.** "This works in most cases, but it depends on the situation" reads more honestly than "This delivers results."

**Mid-sentence qualifications.** "...which, in practice, usually means...", "...at least in most cases...", "...though that probably depends on context." These feel like genuine thinking.

**First person** (when the context allows). "I've seen this work well when..." or "In practice, this often means..." grounds the text.

## Pass 2: Self-audit

After your first rewrite, stop and ask yourself out loud:

> "What still makes this obviously AI-generated?"

Answer briefly. List the remaining tells -- patterns you missed, sentences that still sound clean or robotic, any structure that still feels assembled. Then revise based on your answer.

This step is not optional. The first pass removes the obvious patterns. The second pass catches what survived.

Common survivors:
- Clean parallel pairs you didn't break ("X is one thing. Y is another.")
- Sentences where every clause is the same length
- Paragraphs that all start with a noun + verb
- Still too many clauses starting with "The [thing]..."
- Conclusions that still wrap up too neatly
- Any phrase that could have been written about any topic by swapping a few nouns
- Four paragraphs all roughly the same length
- No concrete numbers, names, or specific details anywhere in the output
- No opinion expressed, even when the context clearly invites one

**Force these questions on your draft:**
- Does every paragraph address exactly one clean point? If yes, break one.
- Does the conclusion tie everything together neatly? If yes, cut or rewrite it. End with a specific fact, a half-formed thought, or just stop.
- Is there any concrete detail (a number, a name, a timeframe) in the text? If no, add at least one.
- Would a real person actually write any of these sentences, or do they sound assembled? Rewrite the assembled-sounding ones.
- Is there an opinion anywhere? If the topic allows for one and there isn't one, add it.

---

## What NOT to change

- The meaning, facts, or sequence of ideas
- Technical terms without plain-language replacements
- The intended audience and purpose

---

## Output

Return only the final rewritten text (after the self-audit pass). No intro, no explanation, no sign-off. Just the content.
